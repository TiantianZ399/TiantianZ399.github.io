Beyond Memory Gating:
Phase as a Missing Axis in Linear-Attention State Transitions

Author Tiantian ZHANGColumbia University Department of Computer Science: why phase/rotation is a distinct design axis from gates, decay, and retention.

Abstract. Three years of linear-attention research have concentrated on the memory-write problem: what to store, how strongly, and how fast to forget. The gating literature is correspondingly mature. We argue that a second axis has been left almost untouched: the spectrum of the state-transition operator itself. Across this family the decay factor is typically a real, nonnegative diagonal, making the recurrence strictly contractive and confining its dynamics to a real spectrum. Such dynamics can compress, retrieve, and forget, but they cannot rotate. Rotation is what periodic, reversible, and modular state tracking require. Semidirect Fourier Delta Attention (SFDA) lifts that diagonal from $\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ to a unit-modulus-scaled complex diagonal: a learnable phase, equivalently a block of $2\times2$ rotations, while preserving the chunkwise kernel path that makes the family efficient.

1. The axis the field has not optimized

A linear-attention layer maintains a matrix memory under an affine recurrence:

\[ S_t = A_t S_{t-1} + B_t . \]

The architectural design space is entirely in $A_t$ and $B_t$. In the KDA family,

\[ S_t = (I-\beta_t k_t k_t^{*})D_tS_{t-1}+\beta_t k_t v_t^{*}, \qquad A_t=(I-\beta_t k_t k_t^{*})D_t . \]

The components have clear roles: $k_t$ is the write location, $v_t$ the content, $\beta_t$ the write strength, and $D_t$ the retention/forgetting schedule. Most recent progress has gone into making the write better: learned gates, adaptive decay, selective updates, and data-dependent retention.

Core claim. The field has heavily optimized the question what should be remembered? The less explored question is how should memory evolve between writes?

The quantity that has not been explored is the eigenstructure of $A_t$ between writes. In this family, $D_t$ is usually a real, nonnegative diagonal. The delta term $I-\beta_t k_t k_t^{*}$ only removes a rank-one component. The state can accumulate, shrink, and be corrected, but it is structurally missing oscillatory modes.

2. What real-spectrum memory cannot do

A real scalar gate $\alpha\in[0,1]$ has no period. No product of such gates implements “advance a counter by one tick modulo $m$,” because that operation is a rotation by $2\pi/m$.

\[ \text{modular tick} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad e^{2\pi i/m}. \]

This is not a tuning issue. It is a property of the operator class. The affected computations include cyclic counters, parity-like modular structure, reversible dynamics, phase synchronization, and semidirect-product automata.

This matters more as linear layers cover more of the stack. If a hybrid model assigns most token mixers to linear memory, the expressivity ceiling of the linear transition becomes the system’s ceiling.

3. SFDA: enrich the transition, keep the kernel

SFDA changes one thing: replace the real decay diagonal with a complex one.

\[ A_t=(I-\beta_t k_t k_t^{*})\Lambda_t, \qquad \Lambda_t=\operatorname{diag}(\alpha_t\odot e^{i\theta_t}) =\bigoplus_i \alpha_{t,i}R(\theta_{t,i}). \]

Each channel now has a magnitude $\alpha_{t,i}$, corresponding to retention, and a phase $\theta_{t,i}$, corresponding to rotation. Setting $\theta_t\equiv0$ recovers KDA exactly.

The non-obvious constraint is kernel compatibility. A dense learned orthogonal control would add rotation but destroy the diagonal-plus-low-rank structure. A complex diagonal keeps every token transition in the form

\[ A_t=\Lambda_t-u_t r_t^{*}, \]

one phase-decay plus one rank-one correction. That is the design: the richest transition family we currently know how to keep inside the KDA-style factorization path.

4. What we have actually shown

Verified algebra. A from-scratch complex-coordinate implementation reproduces the exact claims against brute-force references: constructive chunk-WY product factorization, additive affine chunk-transfer law, KDA reduction at $\theta=0$, exact finite automaton realization including a non-permutation reset, and a contractivity bound under stated conditions.

The phase counter of the cyclic-memory proposition is exact and norm-preserving over $5000$ steps, with residual around $10^{-13}$ in double precision.

5. The falsifiable questions

  1. Does richer transition algebra improve language-model quality at equal compute?
  2. Does it improve long-context retrieval where periodic or positional structure is present?
  3. Can a hybrid carry a higher linear-to-global ratio, such as $7{:}1$ or $15{:}1$, at the quality of a KDA-style $3{:}1$?
  4. Does it maintain KDA-level kernel efficiency?

6. Why this framing

The argument is not that gating was a mistake. Gating produced most of the practical gains in linear attention. The argument is that the gating axis is approaching diminishing returns, while a second axis, the spectrum of the state-transition operator, has mostly remained fixed at the real, contractive case.

SFDA in one line. Add a phase, keep the kernel, recover KDA for free.

The verified algebra and the clean representational separation on modular tasks are evidence that the axis is real. Whether it pays off at scale is the experiment worth running.
