Beyond Memory Gating:
Phase as a Missing Axis in Linear-Attention State Transitions

Tiantian Zhang · Department of Computer Science, Columbia University
Exploring phase-augmented recurrent memory systems and the role of state-transition algebra in next-generation linear attention.

Three years of linear-attention research have largely concentrated on the memory-write problem: what to store, how strongly, and how fast to forget. The gating literature, including RetNet, RWKV, Mamba, DeltaNet, Gated DeltaNet, and KDA, is correspondingly mature.

We argue that a second axis has been left relatively underexplored: the spectrum of the state-transition operator itself. Semidirect Fourier Delta Attention, or SFDA, lifts real diagonal decay to complex or block-rotational phase decay while preserving the chunkwise factorization path that makes KDA-style systems efficient.

Connection to State Tracking in Transformers

A recent DeepMind paper The Topological Trouble With Transformers , argues that purely feedforward Transformer architectures face a structural difficulty with dynamic state tracking. The core issue is not simply context length: the model may retrieve past text, but maintaining an evolving latent state across many updates is a different computational problem.

This perspective is closely aligned with the motivation for SFDA. Current linear-attention and delta-memory systems already introduce recurrence:

\[ S_t=A_tS_{t-1}+B_t. \]

However, in KDA-style systems the transition operator is still largely contractive:

\[ A_t=(I-\beta_t k_tk_t^\top)D_t. \]

This gives the model a mechanism for writing, erasing, and forgetting, but it does not naturally provide oscillatory or reversible state evolution. SFDA asks whether the next bottleneck is not recurrence itself, but the algebra of the recurrent transition.

\[ A_t=(I-\beta_t k_tk_t^\top)\Lambda_t, \qquad \Lambda_t=\operatorname{diag}(\alpha_t e^{i\theta_t}). \]

Under this view, SFDA is one possible response to the state-tracking problem: keep the efficiency of KDA-style recurrent memory, but enrich the internal transition dynamics so the state can rotate, cycle, and represent structured automata rather than only decay.

1. The axis the field has not optimized

A linear-attention memory layer maintains a recurrent matrix state:

\[ S_t = A_t S_{t-1} + B_t . \]

In KDA-style systems, the update can be written as

\[ S_t = (I-\beta_t k_t k_t^\top)D_tS_{t-1} + \beta_t k_t v_t^\top . \]

Here \(k_t\) is the write location, \(v_t\) is the content, \(\beta_t\) is the write strength, and \(D_t\) is the retention or forgetting schedule. Much of the recent field has focused on improving the gate:

\[ \beta_t . \]
The central hypothesis of SFDA is that the next important degree of freedom is not only how much to write, but how memory evolves between writes.

2. What real-spectrum memory cannot do

A real scalar decay \(\alpha \in [0,1]\) has no period. Products of such gates can shrink, retain, or erase information, but they cannot naturally implement rotation. This matters for modular counters, reversible dynamics, phase tracking, and semidirect-product automata.

These tasks require memory to move through state space, not merely accumulate or decay. If a hybrid Transformer asks linear memory to replace many global-attention layers, then the expressive ceiling of the linear transition becomes the system's ceiling.

3. SFDA: enrich the transition, keep the kernel

SFDA replaces the real diagonal decay \(D_t\) with a complex or block-rotational diagonal:

\[ A_t = (I-\beta_t k_t k_t^\top)\Lambda_t, \qquad \Lambda_t = \operatorname{diag}\!\left(\alpha_{t,i}e^{i\theta_{t,i}}\right). \]

Equivalently, in real coordinates:

\[ \Lambda_t = \bigoplus_i \alpha_{t,i} R(\theta_{t,i}), \qquad R(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta\\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}. \]

KDA is recovered when \(\theta_t=0\). Thus SFDA is a strict generalization of KDA, not a separate architecture.

5. What we have actually shown

We separate verified algebra, demonstrated behavior, and open empirical claims.

Verified algebra

  • Constructive chunk-WY product factorization.
  • Associative closure under structured composition.
  • Affine chunk-transfer law.
  • KDA reduction when \(\theta=0\).
  • Exact cyclic counter realization in the phase model.
  • Contractivity bound under standard assumptions.

Minimal synthetic separation

On modular state tracking, the zero-phase KDA reduction cannot represent the cyclic transition, while SFDA can.

6. The falsifiable questions

  1. Does richer transition algebra improve language-model quality at equal compute?
  2. Does it improve long-context retrieval where periodic or positional structure matters?
  3. Can it support higher linear-to-global ratios, such as \(7{:}1\) or \(15{:}1\)?
  4. Can it preserve KDA-level kernel efficiency?

7. Why this framing matters

The claim is not that gating was a mistake. Gating has produced most practical gains in linear attention so far. The claim is that the gating axis may now be reaching diminishing returns, while the spectrum of the state-transition operator has remained mostly fixed at the real, contractive case.
