Beyond Automating Proof

Toward a statistical theory of LLM mathematical discovery: from pretraining distributions and post-training alignment to verifier-induced selection, formal certificates, and the question of what lies beyond proof automation.

1. Why the first slogan was insufficient

The attractive slogan was: an LLM is a high-entropy mathematical search engine, and a verifier stack is a truth-preserving compression filter. It is memorable, but it is not yet a theory.

It hides the most important questions: search with respect to which distribution? truth under which target measure? verification with what soundness? post-training aligned to whose preferences? and what happens when the research problem lies far outside the support of the training data?

Revised thesis. An LLM-based mathematical research system should be modeled as a learned conditional proposal distribution over mathematical objects, proof programs, conjectures, and natural-language explanations. Pretraining estimates statistical regularities of a large data mixture; SFT and preference optimization reshape the policy toward human-desired behavior; test-time search samples candidate trajectories; verifiers tilt or truncate the candidate distribution toward certificates. Formal proof is not a metaphorical filter. It is a certificate mechanism that changes the epistemic status of a generated object.

This reframing is less romantic but more useful. It says that LLMs do not become reliable by sounding mathematical. They become useful when their learned distribution places non-negligible probability mass on certifiable objects, and when the surrounding system can identify those objects without being fooled by fluent but false ones.

2. The statistical object: from data distribution to research distribution

Let P_pre denote the mixture distribution that produced the pretraining corpus: textbooks, papers, code, forum answers, Lean files, problem solutions, informal exposition, and many artifacts that only approximate truth. A decoder-only language model is trained to approximate local conditionals of this distribution:

p_θ(x_t | x_{<t}) ≈ P_pre(x_t | x_{<t}).

But mathematical discovery is not sampled from P_pre. The target object is closer to a latent distribution over certified research artifacts:

z = (definition d, construction c, conjecture g, proof certificate π, explanation e) ∼ P_★.

The research problem is therefore a distribution-shift problem. The model is trained on traces of human mathematics, but we ask it to generate new certified mathematical objects under a target distribution that may be rare, underrepresented, or absent in the corpus.

Key statistical tension. If a valid solution has essentially zero support under the model’s proposal distribution, no verifier can recover it by selection alone. If the model assigns it small but nonzero probability, test-time search and verification can amplify it. Thus the fundamental quantity is not raw intelligence, but coverage of the solution class under the proposal distribution.

In this language, the central question becomes: what features of training data and model architecture make the learned proposal distribution cover rare but valid mathematical structures?

3. Solver–verifier systems as distributional tilting

For a problem prompt x, suppose the model samples candidate trajectories h from a proposal distribution q_θ(h | x). A soft verifier assigns a score V(x,h). Test-time selection can be idealized as an energy tilt:

q_{θ,V,β}(h | x) = q_θ(h | x) exp(β V(x,h)) / Z_β(x).

Here β controls selection pressure. A larger β makes the system more aggressive, but also more vulnerable to verifier overfitting, reward hacking, and spurious high-scoring trajectories.

A hard formal verifier is different. It returns a certificate predicate C(x,h) ∈ {0,1}. The selected distribution is no longer merely preference-weighted; it is truncated to candidates that satisfy the certificate:

q_θ(h | x, C=1) ∝ q_θ(h | x) · 1{C(x,h)=1}.

This is the statistical heart of LLM-assisted formal mathematics. The model supplies proposals; the proof assistant enforces a sound acceptance region.

Search amplification. If one independent proposal succeeds with probability p = Pr_{h∼q_θ}[C(x,h)=1], then N proposals succeed with probability approximately 1-(1-p)^N. The difference between impossible and feasible is often whether p is astronomically small or merely small.

This also clarifies the role of LLM-as-a-Verifier systems. They provide a soft, cheaper scoring function before a hard certificate is available. In code and terminal tasks, logs and tests provide partial observability. In mathematics, Lean, Coq, Isabelle, symbolic computation, exhaustive finite search, and human proof review provide stronger verifiers.

4. What is learned inside the model?

A useful theory should avoid the phrase “the model understands mathematics” unless it specifies an operational meaning. A more disciplined formulation is: the model learns a high-dimensional feature map that compresses statistical regularities of mathematical language, symbolic transformations, proof traces, code, and examples.

Let φ_θ(x) denote the internal representation of a context. This representation may encode reusable features such as:

Local associations theorem names, definitions, common reductions, tactic patterns, and semantic neighbors.
Algorithmic circuits copying, induction-like completion, bracket matching, variable tracking, and proof-state repair.
Proof templates quotient construction, kernel witness, finite model search, “prove two inclusions,” diagram chase, compactness argument.
Counterexample priors small fields, nilpotents, torsion, noncommutativity, characteristic-two cancellation, pathological quotients.

Chen et al.’s subsequence-association view of hallucination fits naturally here. In statistical terms, hallucination is not merely a random error; it is often a high-confidence prediction under a learned proxy feature that is not invariant for the target truth predicate. A subsequence has high association with an output under P_pre, but the full mathematical context requires a rarer association that the model has underlearned.

Hallucination is a failure of learned sufficient statistics: the representation captures a feature predictive in the corpus, but not sufficient for the certificate predicate.

This is why rare mathematical cases are dangerous. The common association may say “integer-valued polynomial rings are closed under multiplication,” while the open problem sits exactly at an exceptional boundary where the common association fails. Discovery begins when the model can propose a boundary object and the verifier can recognize it.

5. SFT, alignment, and the superalignment problem

Pretraining alone estimates a broad text distribution. But frontier assistants are post-trained. Supervised fine-tuning teaches the model to imitate demonstrations of desired behavior. RLHF, DPO, RLAIF, and related methods reshape the base model into a policy that better matches human or AI-evaluated preferences.

For mathematical discovery, this matters because post-training changes the proposal distribution. It may improve instruction following, decomposition, honesty, and tool use. But it may also suppress unusual hypotheses, overfit to polished exposition, or reward persuasive explanations before truth has been certified.

Alignment is not truth. SFT and preference optimization produce behavior that is preferred under a supervision distribution. They do not by themselves identify mathematical truth. In open research, the supervision distribution is weak exactly where the problem is hardest.

This is where the superalignment question appears in a mathematically precise form. If a model produces arguments or conjectures beyond what a human can immediately evaluate, how can weak supervision safely elicit strong capabilities? Mathematics has a special advantage: for many claims, a proof assistant converts a difficult-to-discover result into an easy-to-check certificate. That is a rare case where a potentially superhuman proposal can still be supervised by a mechanically checkable predicate.

But proof assistants do not solve all of research. They check a formal statement against rules. They do not automatically decide which definitions are fruitful, which conjectures are worth pursuing, which abstractions compress a field, or which experimental anomaly deserves attention. Those remain alignment, taste, and scientific-prior problems.

6. Reading pipeline-math correctly

The most interesting lesson of pipeline-math is not that an LLM can “solve open problems” in an oracle sense. A more careful reading is:

The model-generated search distribution, together with human steering and formal verification, found candidate mathematical certificates for certain problems whose solutions could be expressed through structured constructions and checked through Lean or conventional proof review.

The Lean formalizations embody an important anti-hallucination pattern. Definitions are frozen. Target theorem statements are frozen. Proof files must discharge exactly those statements. Scripts ban shortcuts such as sorry, admit, unsafe axioms, or statement drift. This turns informal mathematical generation into a typed certification workflow.

Statistically, this creates a separation between the proposal channel and the acceptance channel. The LLM is allowed to be creative in the proposal channel. The acceptance channel is not creative; it is soundness-biased.

This separation is the real architecture. Without it, fluent mathematical text can be a hallucination. With it, a generated construction can become a certificate-bearing artifact.

7. What is beyond automating proof?

Automating proof assumes the statement is already known. But much of research happens before the theorem statement is fixed. The deeper objective is not only to prove g, but to discover the right g, the right definitions under which g becomes natural, and the right intermediate language in which the proof is short.

Beyond proof automation lies the automation, or augmentation, of the research loop:

Representation discovery finding the coordinate system, abstraction, quotient, category, or finite model in which the problem becomes simple.
Conjecture generation proposing statements with high expected value, not merely high probability of being true.
Counterexample search locating the boundary where a familiar theorem pattern breaks.
Lemma economy choosing intermediate lemmas that minimize proof length and maximize reuse.
Scientific taste ranking which directions deserve attention under limited computation and human time.
Failure mining turning invalid proof states, false conjectures, and verifier failures into future training signal.

Proof automation asks: can the machine finish the derivation? Mathematical discovery asks: can the system choose the right objects so that a derivation exists and becomes visible?

The elegant idea is not that LLMs replace proof. It is that proof becomes the terminal certificate of a broader statistical search over concepts, representations, and conjectures.

8. If a model is incapable, what can make it capable?

In the statistical framing, incapability can arise from several distinct bottlenecks. Each suggests a different intervention.

Support failure The valid object has near-zero probability under q_θ. Intervention: targeted data, synthetic examples, curriculum, retrieval, and library expansion.
Search failure The object has nonzero probability but is too rare. Intervention: tree search, self-play, verifier-guided sampling, decomposition, and tool use.
Verifier failure The scoring function rewards plausibility instead of truth. Intervention: hard certificates, adversarial negatives, calibration, and independent tests.
Representation failure The model lacks the abstraction needed to compress the problem. Intervention: new formal libraries, representation-learning objectives, mechanistic debugging, and expert-designed interfaces.
Alignment failure Post-training rewards polished answers or premature confidence. Intervention: uncertainty calibration, refusal-to-conclude training, proof-state feedback, and process supervision.
Specification failure The target theorem is ambiguous or drifting. Intervention: frozen statements, type-checked definitions, and no-drift gates.

This is the practical research agenda: do not ask only whether the model is smart. Ask which probability mass, verifier signal, abstraction, or supervision channel is missing.

9. A sharper research program

The foundational question can now be stated more cleanly:

What learned representations and post-training procedures make a model’s proposal distribution cover rare but certifiable mathematical objects, and what verifier architecture can select those objects under distribution shift?

This question decomposes into several mathematical subquestions:

  1. Coverage. For what problem classes does q_θ(C=1 | x) become non-negligible? How does coverage scale with data, model size, retrieval, and formal-library exposure?
  2. Selection. Given a noisy verifier V, what is the excess risk of selecting argmax V(x,h_i) from N samples? When does verifier overfitting dominate search gains?
  3. Calibration. Can the model know when its proof sketch is outside its reliable region? Can it express uncertainty in a way useful for proof search?
  4. Identifiability. Which mathematical abstractions are identifiable from text alone, and which require interaction with symbolic environments?
  5. Weak-to-strong supervision. Which certificates allow weak human supervisors to safely elicit stronger-than-human mathematical proposals?
  6. Active data. Which failed proof states, counterexamples, and negative samples provide the highest information gain for future models?

The answer will probably not be a single grand theorem. It may look more like a synthesis of statistical learning theory, information theory, mechanistic interpretability, formal methods, active learning, and alignment.

Final formulation. LLMs do not provide mathematical certainty. They provide a trainable proposal measure over possible mathematical moves. Formal methods provide certificates. Alignment and post-training shape the proposal measure toward useful behavior. The next science is to understand when this composed system discovers new, certifiable structure rather than merely imitating the surface of old proofs.
