Beyond Automating Proof: A Statistical Perspective on LLM-Assisted Mathematical Discovery

This note formulates LLM-assisted mathematics as a composition of learned proposal distributions, post-training alignment, test-time search, verifier-induced selection, and formal certification. The central issue is not whether a model can imitate mathematical prose, but when its induced distribution has sufficient support on rare, certifiable mathematical objects.

Abstract

Recent systems that combine large language models with proof assistants, symbolic tools, and verifier models suggest a research direction broader than proof automation. This note proposes a statistical formulation of that direction. An LLM is treated as a learned conditional proposal distribution over mathematical artifacts: definitions, conjectures, proof sketches, formal proof programs, counterexamples, and explanations. Pretraining determines the initial support and inductive biases of this distribution; supervised fine-tuning and preference optimization reshape it toward human- or system-preferred behavior; test-time search samples from it; and verifiers either softly reweight or hard-truncate the resulting candidates. Within this framework, the main theoretical questions concern support, calibration, verifier reliability, representation learning, and active data generation under distribution shift.

Keywords: large language models; mathematical discovery; proof assistants; verifier models; alignment; proposal distributions; hallucination; active learning.

1. Introduction

A common but imprecise description of LLM-assisted mathematics is that the model acts as a search engine while a verifier filters its outputs. This description captures part of the phenomenon, but it is too informal to serve as a foundation. It does not specify the distribution being searched, the target measure relative to which a solution is rare, the soundness of the verifier, or the effect of post-training on the model’s mathematical behavior.

A more useful formulation is statistical. The model induces a conditional distribution over candidate artifacts. The surrounding system then modifies this distribution through prompting, retrieval, sampling, self-consistency, verifier scoring, formal checking, and human intervention. Mathematical reliability is not obtained from the model alone; it is obtained from the interaction between a proposal mechanism and an acceptance mechanism.

Central question.

What learned representations and post-training procedures make a model’s proposal distribution place non-negligible probability mass on rare but certifiable mathematical objects, and what verifier architecture can select such objects under distribution shift?

This question places LLM-assisted mathematics in continuity with statistical learning theory, active learning, formal methods, and alignment. It also clarifies what may lie beyond automating proof: the discovery of useful definitions, conjectures, reductions, counterexamples, and proof decompositions before a final formal certificate is available.

2. The statistical object

Let x denote a mathematical problem statement or research context, and let h ∈ H denote a candidate artifact. Depending on the task, h may be an informal proof, a Lean term, a counterexample, a lemma graph, a search trajectory, or a proposed definition. An LLM with parameters θ induces a proposal distribution

qθ(h | x).

Pretraining fits local conditionals of a heterogeneous data mixture, which may include textbooks, papers, code, proof traces, forum explanations, incorrect arguments, and redundant derivations. If Ppre denotes this mixture, next-token training approximates conditionals of the form

pθ(xt | x<t) ≈ Ppre(xt | x<t).

However, mathematical discovery concerns a different and much more selective target: a distribution over certified artifacts. In schematic form, one may write

z = (d, g, c, π, e) ∼ P★,

where d is a useful definition, g a conjecture or theorem statement, c a construction or counterexample, π a certificate, and e an explanatory trace. The gap between Ppre and P★ is a distribution-shift problem.

Working hypothesis 1: support is the first-order bottleneck.

If the valid artifact class has essentially zero probability under qθ(·|x), no amount of verifier-based selection can recover it. If the class has small but nonzero probability, then search, decomposition, retrieval, and verification can amplify it. Thus the relevant notion of model capability is not simply fluency or benchmark accuracy, but coverage of the solution class under the proposal distribution.

3. Post-training and alignment

Pretraining alone does not define the behavior of a deployed mathematical assistant. Supervised fine-tuning, reinforcement learning from human feedback, direct preference optimization, constitutional or AI-feedback methods, tool-use training, and domain-specific finetuning all alter the proposal distribution. Let q0 denote the pretrained proposal and qA the post-trained assistant policy. The transformation

q0(h | x) → qA(h | x)

is not merely cosmetic. It changes the probability of admitting uncertainty, decomposing a problem, invoking tools, refusing unsupported claims, producing formal code, and optimizing for concise answers rather than exploratory search.

For mathematical discovery, alignment has a double role. On one hand, it can reduce harmful or misleading behavior, improve instruction following, and make the model easier to use. On the other hand, it can suppress uncertainty, reward polished narratives, and shift probability mass from exploratory but valuable hypotheses toward conventional-sounding answers. This creates a tension between assistant helpfulness and research-level epistemic discipline.

Remark: superalignment as a mathematical-discovery problem.

When models produce arguments or conjectures beyond the immediate competence of human supervisors, the supervision problem becomes structurally similar to weak-to-strong generalization. The supervisor may not be able to judge the object directly, but may be able to judge certificates, local proof obligations, experiments, counterexamples, or formal proof states. This suggests that verifier design is not only an engineering component; it is part of the alignment problem.

4. Verifier-induced selection

Suppose a soft verifier assigns a scalar score V(x,h) to a candidate. Selection by a verifier can be idealized as exponential tilting:

qθ,V,β(h | x) = qθ(h | x) exp(β V(x,h)) / Zβ(x),

where β is the selection strength and Zβ(x) is a normalizing constant. This abstraction captures test-time ranking, best-of-N sampling, self-consistency, and LLM-as-a-verifier methods.

A formal verifier is different. It defines a certificate predicate C(x,h) ∈ {0,1} and an acceptance set

Ax = { h ∈ H : C(x,h)=1 }.

Conditioning on acceptance gives

qθ(h | x, C=1) ∝ qθ(h | x) · 1{C(x,h)=1}.

The distinction is epistemically important. A soft verifier changes ranking; a formal verifier changes the status of a candidate by producing a checkable certificate. In mathematics, Lean, Coq, Isabelle, symbolic computation, exhaustive finite search, and conventional human proof review occupy different points on this spectrum.

Working hypothesis 2: verification converts fluency into evidence only when the acceptance predicate is independent enough from the generator.

If the verifier shares the same failure modes as the generator, selection can amplify hallucination. If the verifier is grounded in a formal semantics, executable test, or independent mathematical check, selection can amplify truth rather than plausibility.

5. Hallucination as proxy-feature failure

The subsequence-association view of hallucination provides a useful bridge between representation learning and mathematical reliability. A model may learn that certain partial patterns are highly predictive in the pretraining distribution, even when those patterns are not invariant for the target truth predicate. In mathematical prose, such patterns may include familiar theorem schemas, common proof endings, standard counterexample templates, or library-specific tactic sequences.

In statistical terms, hallucination can be regarded as prediction from an insufficient statistic. The internal representation φθ(x) captures features that are predictive under Ppre, but the mathematical task requires features that preserve the certificate predicate C. The model then produces an output that is plausible relative to the training distribution but invalid relative to the mathematical semantics.

Hallucination is not simply noise. It is often the consequence of a learned proxy that is correlated with truth in common contexts but fails in rare or adversarial contexts.

This view explains why LLMs can be simultaneously useful and unreliable in research. The same learned associations that generate a false proof can also suggest a promising construction. The difference is whether the surrounding system can distinguish proxy plausibility from certificate validity.

6. Case study: reading pipeline-math as a certificate pipeline

The pipeline-math project is best interpreted not as evidence that an LLM acts as a mathematical oracle, but as a case study in separating proposal generation from acceptance. The repository presents LLM-assisted work on open problems together with Lean formalization artifacts for several commutative-algebra problems. The Lean projects follow a useful pattern: definitions and target theorem statements are fixed, proofs are developed in separate modules, and discharge files check that the proof declarations match the frozen statements.

This design addresses a common failure mode in informal LLM reasoning: statement drift. A model may prove a related but weaker theorem, silently add assumptions, or change definitions. By freezing definitions and theorem statements, a formal pipeline makes this drift visible. By banning unchecked shortcuts and requiring proof-assistant compilation, it creates a hard acceptance region.

Proposal–certificate decomposition.

The LLM, human researcher, retrieval system, and search procedures belong to the proposal channel. Lean files, theorem statements, proof terms, no-drift gates, and axiom audits belong to the acceptance channel. The reliability of the whole system depends on maintaining this separation.

From the statistical perspective, pipeline-math suggests that LLMs may be especially useful for open problems whose solutions have compact certificates: explicit counterexamples, finite constructions, algebraic objects with checkable properties, or proof decompositions that can be formalized. This does not imply universal open-problem solving; it identifies a class of problems where proposal probability and verifier strength may be favorably matched.

7. What is beyond automating proof?

Proof automation usually assumes that the theorem statement is already fixed. Mathematical research often begins earlier. The central difficulty may be to identify the right objects, definitions, reductions, examples, and intermediate lemmas. A system that only completes proofs addresses the terminal stage of discovery, not the full research process.

Beyond proof automation, the relevant tasks include:

  1. Representation discovery: finding a coordinate system, quotient, category, normalization, or finite model in which the problem becomes tractable.
  2. Conjecture formation: proposing statements with high expected research value, not merely high surface plausibility.
  3. Counterexample search: locating boundary cases where standard intuitions or common theorem schemas fail.
  4. Lemma economy: choosing intermediate statements that minimize formal proof complexity and maximize future reuse.
  5. Failure mining: converting failed proof states, invalid examples, and verifier rejections into training or retrieval signal.
  6. Scientific taste: allocating limited computational and human effort among many plausible but unequal directions.

Thus the deeper objective is not simply to automate the derivation π : g. It is to assist the discovery of d, g, c, and the proof architecture under which π becomes short and checkable.

8. Failure modes and interventions

The statement “the model cannot solve the problem” is too coarse. Different failure modes require different interventions.

Failure mode Description Possible intervention
Support failure The valid artifact has near-zero probability under the proposal distribution. Targeted data, synthetic examples, retrieval, formal-library expansion, curriculum design.
Search failure The valid artifact has nonzero probability but is too rare to sample directly. Tree search, decomposition, self-play, verifier-guided sampling, tool use.
Verifier failure The scoring function rewards plausibility rather than certificate validity. Hard verifiers, adversarial negatives, calibration, independent tests, formal semantics.
Representation failure The model lacks the abstraction needed to compress the problem. New interfaces, mechanistic analysis, representation-learning objectives, expert-designed decompositions.
Alignment failure Post-training rewards polished or overconfident answers over epistemic discipline. Process supervision, uncertainty calibration, proof-state feedback, refusal-to-conclude behavior.
Specification failure The target problem is ambiguous or changes during the interaction. Frozen definitions, theorem statements, formal problem interfaces, no-drift gates.

This taxonomy suggests a practical methodology: treat each failed attempt as information about which component of the system lacks the needed signal.

9. Research questions

The preceding formulation leads to several concrete theoretical questions.

  1. Coverage and scaling. For which classes of mathematical problems does Prh∼qθ[C(x,h)=1] become non-negligible? How does this probability scale with data, model size, retrieval, formal proof traces, and domain finetuning?
  2. Verifier risk. Given a noisy verifier V and N sampled candidates, what is the risk of selecting a high-scoring but invalid artifact? When does verifier overfitting dominate the gain from test-time scaling?
  3. Calibration under distribution shift. Can a model reliably estimate when a proposed proof or conjecture lies outside the region where its learned associations are trustworthy?
  4. Identifiability of abstractions. Which mathematical abstractions are learnable from text alone, and which require interaction with formal systems, computation, or expert feedback?
  5. Active data selection. Which failed proof states, counterexamples, and negative trajectories provide maximal information for improving future proposal distributions?
  6. Weak-to-strong supervision. How can weaker human supervisors safely guide systems that may generate stronger-than-human candidate proofs or conjectures, provided that local certificates are available?
  7. Beyond proof certificates. What forms of certificate can validate conjecture quality, representation choice, or research taste before a final theorem is proved?

The likely theory will not be a single theorem. It will combine statistical learning theory, information theory, mechanistic interpretability, formal semantics, active learning, and alignment. The key object is the composed system: data distribution, model architecture, post-training procedure, search algorithm, verifier, and feedback loop.
