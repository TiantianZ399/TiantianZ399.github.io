A research note on LLM-assisted mathematical discovery.

Abstract

Recent systems for LLM-assisted mathematics suggest a research program broader than proof automation. Frontier models are not merely generating Lean tactics or filling proof gaps; they are beginning to participate in the production of definitions, conjectures, counterexamples, reductions, lemma decompositions, proof sketches, formalization interfaces, and research narratives.

A useful mathematical abstraction is to view the model as inducing a conditional proposal distribution over candidate mathematical objects,

\[ q_\theta(h\mid x), \]

where \(x\) is a problem context and \(h\) may be a proof sketch, Lean program, counterexample, theorem statement, definition, conjecture, experiment, or research trajectory. Pretraining determines the effective support and inductive bias of this proposal distribution. Post-training reshapes its behavioral policy. Trajectory rollouts expose the model to feedback-rich environments. Creative generation mechanisms determine whether the model can sample diverse and globally coherent candidates. Soft verifiers reweight candidates; hard verifiers such as Lean, Coq, Isabelle, symbolic computation, executable tests, finite search, and human mathematical review impose certificates.

This note argues that the central question is not whether language models are “smart enough” in a vague sense. A more precise question is:

What data distributions, internal representations, post-training objectives, trajectory environments, creative proposal mechanisms, search procedures, and verifier architectures make rare but certifiable mathematical objects discoverable?

The discussion connects five lines of work: prover-verifier pipelines such as pipeline-math; AI4Math systems such as Rethlas/Archon; formal proving agents such as Aristotle; verifier-based trajectory selection; hallucination as misweighted subsequence association; and algorithmic-creativity results suggesting that next-token prediction may be myopic for tasks requiring latent global planning.

1. Why “LLM as Search Engine” Is Not Enough

A common description says that the model proposes candidate proofs and the verifier filters them. This is directionally right but theoretically incomplete. It leaves unspecified what distribution is being sampled, what target distribution mathematical research requires, how post-training changes model behavior, how interactive trajectories alter the data regime, and how language-level plausibility becomes mathematical validity.

Given a mathematical problem context \(x\), an LLM should not be modeled as directly emitting truth. It induces a distribution

\[ q_\theta(h\mid x) \]

over candidate objects \(h\in\mathcal H\): informal proofs, formal proofs, Lean programs, counterexamples, theorem statements, definitions, lemma graphs, symbolic computations, tool-use traces, or explanations. A research system succeeds when the target object lies in the effective support of this distribution and when search, creative sampling, retrieval, trajectory feedback, and verification amplify the relevant probability mass.

The research question is therefore not merely “Can a model prove the theorem?” It is: what makes the probability of sampling a certifiable object non-negligible under realistic compute, search, and verification budgets?

2. Statistical Objects: From Pretraining Distribution to Certificate Distribution

Let \(P_{\mathrm{pre}}\) denote the pretraining mixture. It may contain textbooks, papers, code, formal proofs, forum discussions, incorrect arguments, repeated derivations, incomplete drafts, and noisy explanations. Next-token training fits conditional regularities of the form

\[ P_{\mathrm{pre}}(x_t\mid x_{<t}). \]

Mathematical discovery, however, asks for a much sparser target distribution. One can write an idealized target as

\[ P_\star(d,g,c,\pi,e\mid x), \]

where \(d\) denotes useful definitions, \(g\) intermediate goals or conjectures, \(c\) constructions or counterexamples, \(\pi\) proofs or formal certificates, and \(e\) explanatory trajectories. The fundamental problem is distribution shift: \(P_{\mathrm{pre}}\) is not \(P_\star\). A model can be strong on common mathematical prose and still lack support on rare constructions, adversarial counterexamples, or formal certificates.

A useful principle is:

If the solution class has nearly zero probability under \(q_\theta(\cdot\mid x)\), a verifier cannot recover it. If the probability is small but nonzero, decomposition, retrieval, search, creative sampling, trajectory rollout, and verification may amplify it.

Thus model capability should be described not only by benchmark accuracy, but by coverage, sampleability, diversity, originality, and selectability by verifiers.

3. Scaling Laws, Transformer, MoE, and Data Distribution

Industry discussions often reduce frontier model progress to three ingredients: Transformer, MoE, and scaling laws. This slogan is useful only if made precise.

The Transformer provides a parallelizable architecture for sequence modeling. MoE increases total parameter capacity through sparse activation while controlling per-token active compute. Scaling laws describe empirical power-law relationships between loss and model size, data size, and training compute. Kaplan et al. studied language-model loss as a function of model size, dataset size, and compute; Chinchilla-style results emphasized that compute-optimal training requires scaling parameters and tokens together rather than increasing parameters alone.

A more rigorous abstraction is:

\[ \text{capability} \approx F(\text{architecture},\text{parameters},\text{data},\text{compute},\text{optimization},\text{post-training},\text{inference-time search}). \]

Algorithms matter, but often by making scale and feedback usable: stabilizing optimization, enabling larger sparse models, improving data quality, lowering inference cost, and converting environment feedback into learnable signals. For mathematical discovery, the crucial question is not simply how many tokens or parameters are scaled, but which distributions are scaled: static mathematical text, reasoning traces, Lean proof states, theorem-search logs, compiler failures, trajectory rollouts, or verified certificates.

The rigorous version of the scaling argument is therefore:

Mathematical discovery depends on scaling not just data volume, but the right mixture of text, reasoning, trajectories, formal states, and certificates.

Brand-specific claims about closed models, internal parameter counts, or proprietary data mixtures should be treated cautiously unless backed by public ablations or documentation. The theory should be stated at the level of distributions and environments, not rumors about individual model families.

4. From Static Text to Trajectory Rollouts

A major shift in language-model systems is the move from chatbot interaction to agentic environments. In such environments, a model does not merely emit text; it takes actions, observes feedback, and continues. A trajectory can be written as

\[ \tau=(s_0,a_0,o_1,r_1,s_1,a_1,o_2,r_2,\ldots), \]

with approximate distribution

\[ P(\tau)=P(s_0)\prod_t \pi_\theta(a_t\mid h_t)E(o_{t+1},r_{t+1},s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t), \]

where \(E\) is the environment and \(h_t\) is the model-visible history.

For a coding agent, the environment may include the command line, Python, unit tests, a browser, a repository, or a compiler. For a mathematical agent, the environment may include Lean, Coq, Isabelle, a CAS, an SMT solver, a finite model finder, a theorem search engine, or a paper retrieval system.

A useful concept is verifier density: the degree to which an environment supplies cheap, objective, frequent feedback for local actions. Proof assistants are high-verifier-density environments because each tactic, lemma, import, and definition can be checked by a type checker. Informal mathematical research is lower-density because many conceptual moves cannot be locally certified.

This clarifies the AlphaGo analogy. Go is a closed game with exact rules and a clear self-play reward. Computer-use and theorem-proving environments are semi-closed: they have executable feedback, but their goals, semantic background, and task spaces are more open. The right lesson is not that agent training escapes scaling laws. Rather:

Trajectory rollout changes what kind of data is being scaled: from static text tokens to interaction traces in feedback-rich environments.

For mathematics, the relevant data families include static papers and textbooks, distilled reasoning traces, tool-use trajectories, proof assistant states, theorem DAGs, tactic scripts, failed formalization attempts, and verified certificates. A strong AI-for-math system may arise from closing the loop among these distributions: text supplies priors, trajectories supply feedback, formal states supply precision, and certificates supply truth.

5. Post-Training, Alignment, and the Shape of the Proposal Distribution

Pretraining is not the whole story. SFT, RLHF, RLAIF, DPO-style preference optimization, rejection sampling, tool-use training, safety training, and long-CoT distillation modify the proposal distribution:

\[ q_{\theta_0}(h\mid x) \longrightarrow q_{\theta_{\mathrm{post}}}(h\mid x). \]

These procedures may improve instruction following, decomposition, self-correction, tool use, and verifier-facing explanations. They may also bias a model toward polished but conventional answers, discourage risky conjectural exploration, or reward surface plausibility over mathematical discoverability.

This creates a special version of the weak-to-strong supervision problem. If human supervisors or LLM verifiers are too weak to judge deep mathematical correctness, post-training may optimize the wrong signal. Alignment for mathematical discovery should therefore optimize calibrated uncertainty, search diversity, certificate-seeking behavior, and willingness to expose unresolved proof obligations.

A research-oriented model should naturally say: “this lemma is still unproved,” “this construction works in finite tests but lacks a general proof,” “this step needs formal verification,” or “the current library lacks the required abstraction.” Scientific alignment is not the same as ordinary chatbot helpfulness.

6. Creative Proposal Distributions

Verification is only useful after there is something worth verifying. The paper Roll the dice & look before you leap highlights a generator-side bottleneck: standard autoregressive next-token learning may be structurally myopic for tasks requiring implicit stochastic planning before generation.

Autoregressive generation factors as

\[ q_\theta(y\mid x)=\prod_{t=1}^T q_\theta(y_t\mid x,y_{<t}). \]

Open-ended creativity may require first choosing a latent global plan,

\[ q_\theta(y\mid x)=\int q_\theta(z\mid x)\prod_{t=1}^T q_\theta(y_t\mid x,z,y_{<t})\,dz, \]

where \(z\) is a latent construction, analogy bridge, seed, proof strategy, or counterexample template.

In the algorithmic creativity tasks of that paper, teacherless multi-token training and diffusion-style methods produce more diverse and original outputs than standard next-token learning. Input-level randomness through hash-conditioning is often more effective than merely raising the output temperature because it allows randomness to select a coherent latent plan before local generation begins.

For mathematical discovery, this matters. A useful counterexample, analogy, theorem decomposition, finite shadow, or proof path is not simply a locally smooth continuation of text. It often requires a global choice of representation. Thus the pipeline should be extended:

\[ \text{scaling/data}\rightarrow\text{post-training}\rightarrow\text{trajectory rollout}\rightarrow\text{creative proposal distribution}\rightarrow\text{search}\rightarrow\text{verifier selection}\rightarrow\text{certificate}. \]

The key question is not only how to verify a candidate, but how to train a model to propose candidates worth verifying.

7. pipeline-math: From Informal Discovery to Formal Certificate

The pipeline-math repository is important because it places LLM-generated mathematical reasoning inside an auditable prover-verifier pipeline. The core abstraction is not that the LLM is trusted as an oracle, but that a generated proposal is mapped into formal proof obligations.

A typical flow is:

open problem
  → candidate construction / proof sketch
  → frozen definitions and theorem statements
  → modular proof obligations
  → Lean formalization
  → no-statement-drift checks
  → sorry / axiom / unsafe audit
  → human-readable theorem

The crucial design is the frozen semantic interface. Definitions and theorem statements are fixed before proof files are accepted. Discharge gates check that proof declarations match the frozen theorem statements. Verification scripts audit for sorry, axioms, unsafe definitions, and statement drift. In statistical terms, the system maps a raw proposal \(h\) to a certificate predicate

\[ C(x,h)\in\{0,1\}. \]

Lean acceptance is a hard certificate rather than linguistic plausibility. The LLM is a proposal mechanism; the proof assistant, symbolic computation, finite search, theorem proving, and expert review are the certificate stack.

8. AI4Math, Rethlas/Archon, and Aristotle

Recent public systems show that AI-for-math is becoming an architecture question rather than a single-model question.

AI4Math’s Anderson conjecture project separates an informal reasoning agent, Rethlas, from a formal verification agent, Archon. Rethlas uses the theorem search engine Matlas to find relevant mathematical results and construct candidate proofs. Archon uses LeanSearch and structured decomposition to turn informal arguments into Lean 4 projects. The authors report resolving an open problem in commutative algebra proposed by D. D. Anderson and verifying the result in Lean 4. The corresponding repository describes a Lean formalization of the statement that there exists a weakly quasi-complete Noetherian local ring that is not quasi-complete, with roughly 20,000 lines of Lean across 44 files.

The significance is not that an AI system has replaced mathematicians. It is that a system combining semantic theorem retrieval, informal proposal generation, task decomposition, formal proof synthesis, and hard verification can discover and certify a nontrivial counterexample. The architecture is the message:

semantic retrieval → informal proof blueprint → formal task decomposition → Lean synthesis → certificate

Harmonic’s Aristotle represents a related direction: a long-horizon formal theorem-proving agent that combines informal, human-like reasoning with Lean 4 verification and Monte Carlo-style proof search. Public materials report gold-medal-level performance on the 2025 IMO formal problems and emphasize autonomous proving/formalization over long time horizons. These claims should still be read with benchmark and reporting caveats, but they indicate a clear trend:

Formal theorem proving systems are becoming long-horizon agents, not merely tactic predictors.

For theory, the important object is the proof-search trajectory: proof state, action, Lean feedback, repair, lemma invention, new proof state, and eventually a certificate or failure trace. This is precisely the kind of data that can make future models better.

9. Soft Verifiers Before Hard Certificates

LLM-as-a-Verifier frameworks are not formal theorem provers. They provide soft trajectory selection. Given many candidate trajectories, a verifier model scores them using decomposed criteria, repeated verification, and score-token log probabilities. This can be modeled as verifier-induced reweighting:

\[ q_{\theta,V,\beta}(h\mid x)\propto q_\theta(h\mid x)\exp(\beta V(x,h)). \]

When \(V\) is an LLM judge, this is soft and fallible. When \(V\) is Lean acceptance, an executable test, or exact computation, it approaches hard truncation:

\[ q_\theta(h\mid x,C=1). \]

A robust mathematical discovery system should use multiple layers: generator, trajectory environment, soft verifier, hard verifier, and human expert judgment. The soft verifier should reduce search cost; it should not replace formal correctness.

10. Hallucination as Misweighted Association

The subsequence-association view of hallucination explains why the generator is useful but unreliable. A decoder-only transformer may learn many partial input-output associations; when a hallucinatory association dominates the faithful global structure, the model emits a plausible but false statement.

Mathematics is full of such traps. Associations like “integer-valued polynomial ring implies closure under multiplication,” “canonical tensor product map is likely an isomorphism,” or “finite-conductor hypotheses imply nice coherence behavior” may be correct in common regimes but false near the boundary of an open problem.

Combined with the creativity discussion, the generator-side theory becomes:

Hallucination is local association without truth discipline; weak creativity is local prediction without latent global planning; weak agency is action generation without feedback-calibrated trajectories.

Verification filters bad candidates, but a theory of mathematical discovery must also explain how candidate generation goes wrong and how training, retrieval, trajectory feedback, and formal environments can correct it.

11. What Internal Structure Do SOTA LLMs Learn?

A plausible hypothesis is that LLMs learn a compressed, entangled, probabilistic atlas of mathematical text, code, proof motifs, symbolic transformations, formalization patterns, and interactive trajectories. The internal objects are not literal theorem databases. They are mixtures of associative fragments, algorithmic circuits, proof templates, counterexample priors, library priors, trajectory priors, and latent plan priors.

Mechanistic interpretability provides early tools for studying this: induction heads, superposition, sparse features, and circuit analysis. But mathematical discovery needs task-specific interpretability. We need to know which features correspond to algebraic construction patterns, which circuits support symbolic binding, which subsequences trigger false lemma hallucinations, and which trajectory patterns teach proof-state repair.

This cannot be inferred from benchmark accuracy alone. It requires linking interpretability, data distribution, trajectory analysis, formal verification, and failure mining.

12. Why Some Open Problems Become Accessible

LLM-assisted systems are more likely to help with open problems that have favorable structure:

  1. The answer admits a compact construction, counterexample, finite witness, or proof DAG.
  2. The ingredients already exist in literature, code, or formal libraries.
  3. The difficulty is recombination rather than the creation of an entirely new theory.
  4. Wrong candidates can be rejected cheaply.
  5. The final result can be checked by Lean, computation, or experts.
  6. The proposal distribution assigns non-negligible mass to the right structure.
  7. Creative sampling produces diverse candidates rather than minor variants of common templates.
  8. The trajectory environment supplies dense enough feedback for repair.

Counterexample-driven mathematics is therefore especially suitable for early LLM-assisted discovery: it often has compact witnesses and locally verifiable properties.

Failures are more informative when decomposed. A failed attempt may indicate support failure, search failure, creative proposal failure, trajectory failure, verifier failure, retrieval failure, representation failure, alignment failure, specification failure, library failure, curriculum failure, or significance failure.

13. Failure Modes and Interventions

Failure mode Meaning Possible intervention
Support failure The correct structure has near-zero mass under \(q_\theta\) Targeted data, retrieval, library expansion, domain finetuning
Search failure The correct structure has small mass but is not sampled Best-of-N, tree search, theorem DAG decomposition, parallel sampling
Creative proposal failure Candidates lack diversity or originality Multi-token training, diffusion, seed-conditioning, latent-plan prompting
Trajectory failure The agent has too little interaction data or sparse feedback Lean/Python/CLI rollouts, richer environments, tool feedback
Data-distribution failure The corpus contains text but few proof-state, tool-use, or failure-repair trajectories Synthetic formal tasks, environment rollouts, curated negative data, certificate datasets
Infrastructure failure The model lacks scalable retrieval, Lean environments, kernel checks, or library APIs Matlas/LeanSearch-style theorem search, reproducible Lean projects, library engineering
Scale-misdiagnosis failure Capability is attributed to parameter count alone while distribution, feedback, and verification are ignored Public ablations, model cards, data-regime analysis, environment-level evaluation
Verifier failure Wrong candidates are accepted or right ones rejected Stronger verifiers, adversarial tests, formal certificates, calibration
Retrieval failure A key theorem or paper is missed Semantic theorem search, citation-graph search, human seed hints
Representation failure The problem is encoded poorly Better definitions, finite shadows, alternate encodings
Alignment failure Post-training rewards fluency over research exploration Research-oriented SFT/RLHF, uncertainty calibration, failure-trace data
Specification failure The theorem statement drifts or is wrong Frozen interfaces, theorem stubs, discharge gates, expert review
Library failure The formal ecosystem lacks needed abstractions Mathlib expansion, lemma mining, proof repair logs
Curriculum failure The model lacks nearby proof/counterexample trajectories Active data, synthetic formal tasks, solver-verifier self-play
Significance failure The result is formally correct but unimportant Human taste, problem selection, novelty filters

The practical question after a failure is therefore: which bottleneck failed, and what data, search, environment, verifier, or library intervention would move the target object into reach?

14. Toward a Closed-Loop Research System

A complete LLM mathematical discovery system should be a closed loop:

pretraining / post-training / synthetic data
        ↓
agent trajectory rollouts and proof-state data
        ↓
creative proposal model qθ(h|x)
        ↓
seeded / multi-token / diffusion-style sampling
        ↓
theorem retrieval / tool use / environment interaction
        ↓
soft verifier ranking
        ↓
formal verification / computation / finite search
        ↓
certificate or failure trace
        ↓
failure mining / active data / library expansion
        ↓
updated model, tools, and formal ecosystem

In this loop, failures are not discarded. Lean errors, failed proof states, wrong counterexamples, overstrong statements, missing coercions, unavailable lemmas, bad retrieval paths, and false analogy traces become active data.

The human researcher’s role shifts from merely checking final proofs to designing the right decomposition, formal interface, verifier stack, retrieval corpus, trajectory environment, curriculum, and notion of significance.

15. What Is Beyond Automating Proof?

If proof automation means “given a theorem, find a proof,” then beyond proof automation includes definition discovery, conjecture generation, counterexample search, analogy discovery, representation design, formal interface design, trajectory-environment design, creative proposal training, verifier-aware learning, failure-to-data conversion, and human-AI research methodology.

A concise formulation is:

Beyond automating proof is the study of how statistical models, trajectory environments, creative proposal mechanisms, formal verifiers, and human mathematical taste can form a reliable discovery process.

This is a different objective from making a model sound like a mathematician. The aim is to build an infrastructure in which models propose, environments respond, verifiers discipline, failures become curriculum, libraries become shared memory, and humans guide abstraction and significance.

16. A Sharpened Research Question

Given a problem context \(x\), candidate space \(\mathcal H\), model proposal distribution \(q_\theta(h\mid x)\), latent plan distribution \(q_\theta(z\mid x)\), trajectory environment \(E\), soft verifier \(V(x,h)\), and certificate predicate \(C(x,h)\), we want to understand

\[ p_B(x)=\Pr[\exists h\in\mathcal S_B(x):C(x,h)=1], \]

where \(\mathcal S_B(x)\) is the candidate set produced under search budget \(B\). Under a crude independent-sampling approximation, if one sample succeeds with probability \(p\), then

\[ p_B\approx 1-(1-p)^B. \]

The real research problem is that \(p\) is endogenous. It depends on pretraining support, model scale, post-training, next-token versus multi-token objectives, seed-conditioning, retrieval, tool use, theorem DAG decomposition, verifier calibration, formal library coverage, failure-trace feedback, and human abstraction design.

This decomposition is more productive than asking whether a model can solve open problems in the abstract. It yields components that can be measured, analyzed, and improved.

17. Conclusion

pipeline-math illustrates a hard-verifier mathematical discovery pipeline. AI4Math’s Rethlas/Archon illustrates theorem retrieval plus informal reasoning plus formal verification. Aristotle illustrates long-horizon formal proving agents. LLM-as-a-Verifier illustrates soft trajectory selection. Hallucination theory explains why generators are misled by local associations. Algorithmic-creativity work shows that generators may need latent global planning beyond next-token prediction.

Together these lines of work suggest a broader scientific program:

Build systems where models generate diverse and original mathematical candidates, trajectory environments provide feedback, verifiers impose truth, failures become curriculum, formal libraries become shared memory, and human researchers guide abstraction and significance.

This is the deeper meaning of LLMs for mathematics: not language-level imitation of mathematicians, but a new discovery infrastructure combining statistical learning, trajectory rollout, creative generation, formal methods, and human taste.
